# 🏗️ The Tool That Builds Your Tools

Up until now, you have been building agents by hand. One by one: SPOKE™ for a persona, and then a chain in SPOKEN™ when you needed two links. It works, but it is also starting to become a lot of work. In this chapter, we are making a completely different move: instead of building every agent by hand, we are building one agent that builds all the rest. And before we explain how, there is a short story that explains why.

**What you will know how to do by the end of this chapter**
Feed the SPOKE™ / SPOKEN™ method to Claude and challenge it, ask it for a research plan on how the world actually builds agents, run the research in several tools simultaneously and gather the results, and then use them to build an agent named Banay that builds all your other agents. In the end, you will not just have another agent in your hands, but your own personal agent factory.

**Before this chapter:** Agents (you built a SPOKE™ agent), Chains (the letter N of SPOKEN™), and Deep Research from this very session (you came out of it with your research skill built and a few research tools organized in your favorites). This chapter simply connects the three into one move, it is not a new framework.

**Reading time:** About twenty minutes, a significant part of it in front of the screen.
**What you should have open:** A Claude account, the Deep Research skill installed, and access to at least two or three research tools (the ones you organized together earlier).

**Chapter map:** First, the story that starts it all, and the big transition it marks. Then, the first cycle, feeding the method to Claude and challenging it. Second cycle, spreading the research to the world and gathering it. And finally, building at home: building Banay in two steps, with a full guiding prompt that generates a strong agent.

## The Story That Starts It All

Before we dive in, it is important to me to share where this whole thing came from, because without the story, this unit feels arbitrary.

I needed to build an agent. At the time, I was using a workspace called BMad, and inside it was an agent that builds agents, a sort of Agent Builder. I went to it, tried to build one, and the agent that came out was really mediocre. And that bothered me. I sat and thought, and said one sentence to myself that opened this whole door: It cannot be that the whole world is building agents, people are using AI and know how to build excellent agents, and only I do not. I must understand how people in the world actually build agents.

You do not have to have your own pain point for this to feel real. Take this as a story that explains why this unit exists: one small failure sent me on a research journey, and from that research, the agent I am most proud of was born. You are going to build it.

## The Big Transition: From "I Built an Agent" to "I Built a Tool That Builds My Tools"

One point needs to land before we move on. SPOKE™ and SPOKEN™ are excellent methods, and they work. But they are short and simple, built for a single agent or a short chain. As you want more complex agents, those that live on several different platforms, maybe a Telegram bot, maybe something running in the background, the complexity rises, and doing it by hand requires something stronger.

So instead of building every such agent by hand, we make a different move: we build one agent that builds all the rest. And we do not base it on what we already know, but on the proven methods in the world that we will gather through research. This is the transition. From "I built an agent" to "I built a tool that builds my tools."

It is important to understand that this is not a departure from what we learned. SPOKE™ and SPOKEN™ remain the foundation, they are the fuel. We are simply adding another floor on top of them. And why are you building this yourself instead of getting mine ready-made? Because the whole point is to build, not to watch. Once you build it, this meta-agent will build everything else for you.

There is one way to look at this entire move. SPOKE™ and SPOKEN™ are the multiplication table, and Banay is the calculator you build for yourself. You need to know the multiplication table before you touch a calculator, and from today on, you really will not do the work by hand, the calculator will. But your difference from other people is that you also know it in your head: on the day you land on a new platform that you want to try right then and there, without your calculator within reach, you will still know how to build an agent yourself.

## Cycle 1: Feeding the Method to Claude and Challenging It

### The Idea

The move begins with humility. You take the method you already have, SPOKE™ and SPOKEN™, tell Claude about it, and then tell it honestly: I do not think this method is good enough, I am sure people in the world build agents in better, proven ways. This is the callback that connects everything we have learned. You are not starting from scratch, you are taking what you have and asking the world how to do it better.

And then, in the same breath, you ask it not to answer off the top of its head, but to write a research plan for you to run in several tools simultaneously. This is exactly where what you just learned in Deep Research comes in: the method is that we always go to the world before we decide what is right.

### How It Looks

You open a new conversation in Claude, and do two things in it. First, you explain the method to it, letter by letter: S is Self, who the agent is, the role, and the audience. P is Principles, three to five working principles. O is Outputs, what it receives as input and what it returns as output. K is Knowledge, what the agent must know about your world. E is Execution, how it works step-by-step. And the N of SPOKEN™, which declares that the agent is a link in a chain. Then, immediately after, you challenge it, ask for the proven methods in the world, and ask it to write a research plan for the Deep Research skill.

A good question that comes up here is a question about pedagogy, not just about the tool: if we want people to know how to express themselves to AI, why are we handing out a ready-made prompt? The answer is that people first need to see what good phrasing looks like, try it, feel that it works, and only then do they understand how to do it themselves. The ready-made prompt is not a crutch, it is an example. So take it, run it, and feel how it is written.

Here is the ready-made prompt for this stage, to copy. Paste it into Claude and run it:

```
I have a method for building agents that I call SPOKE / SPOKEN:
[Here, paste the description of the method - the five components S/P/O/K/E + the letter N].

I do not think this method is good enough. I am sure people in the world
build agents in better, proven ways. I want to know what the proven
methods for building agents in the world are - what is the way to build agents
so that they always work, always perform, always complete tasks, according to
a correct structure that fits AI.

I want to do a Deep Research on this using my DR skill.
Write a structured research plan for me to run in several tools in parallel, to get
a complete answer on the most correct ways to build an agent. Explain what you understood
and wait for approval to continue.
```

Pay attention to the end of the prompt: "Explain what you understood and wait for approval." This is the same anchor from SPOKE™. You want it to stop, show you that it understood, and only then output the plan. And in which language should the research be written? In English, always. That is how you meet the most sources.

### Now You

Open a new conversation in Claude, feed it the full SPOKE™ / SPOKEN™ method, challenge it, and ask for a research plan for the Deep Research skill. Within a few moments, you will have a ready research plan in hand.

### Self-Check

How will you know it turned out well? When the plan that came back is organized, with a main question, sub-questions, and a clear scope, and not just a single general paragraph. If the plan that came back is too thin, ask it to add sub-questions and expand the scope, just like you did in Deep Research.

> **In your own words:** Why do we challenge our method instead of simply using it?
> *(Example answer: Because SPOKE / SPOKEN are short and simple, and for a complex agent that lives on several platforms, we want to base it on the proven methods in the world, not just on what we already know.)*

## Cycle 2: Spreading the Research to the World and Gathering It

### The Idea

Now you take the research plan and spread it across several tools in parallel. This is the first application of what you learned in Deep Research: do not run it in one place, but in several, and then leave it to run in the background and move on.

And why really in several places and not just in one? Because each research tool has access to different corners of the internet, and even if they all have the same information, their brain is different, so each one goes to different places. When building a large database on something, you want it to rely on a variety of sources, not a single source. If you have always done Deep Research from one place and that was enough, try it differently this time, this is what will level you up.

### How It Looks

You open tab after tab: Perplexity, ChatGPT, Gemini, Claude, and a few more of your favorites. In each one, you paste the same research plan, click Deep Research, and run it. Some of the tools ask clarifying questions before they start, you answer them briefly and let them run. Even a tool that only has regular web search and not full Deep Research, you run that too, it still brings sources.

And here is the heart of the matter: you cannot sit and wait. You click run, and leave it. It runs in the background, between a few minutes and twenty, and meanwhile, you work on something else. One thing runs in the background, and you work on another. This is what will level you up.

When an answer comes back, you collect it. You open a new Project named Building a Meta-Agent, and into its Files you paste each result as it comes back, one after the other, each with the name of the tool it came from. This way, all the research accumulates in one place, ready for Banay.

An honest note: this takes time, and logistically it is a bit annoying to open ten tabs. Three or four tools is excellent. Do not get stuck on the number, get stuck on the principle: multiple sources, running in the background, collecting as you go.

### Now You

Take the research plan, open three or four tools, paste it into each, run it, and leave it. Open a Project named Building a Meta-Agent, and as each result comes back, paste it into its Files with the name of the tool. Do not sit in front of the screen, let it run.

### Self-Check

How will you know you are on track? When there are several tools running simultaneously, and you are not sitting and looking at any of them but are busy with something else. The moment you catch yourself waiting in front of a screen, stop, that is exactly what not to do.

**Common mistake:** Running in one place and waiting there, or opening all the tools and then sitting and staring. The entire power is in running and leaving.

> **In your own words:** Why do we run the same plan in several tools and not just in one?
> *(Example answer: Because each tool has a different brain and accesses different sources, so several tools provide a broad and diverse database instead of a single answer from a single source.)*

## The Power Level: Lightweight Agent vs. Strong Agent

Before moving on to the build, it is important to understand one distinction, because it is the heart of this unit.

Up until now, you have built lightweight agents. In SPOKE™, you filled in five letters with a few sentences and got a working agent, quick as a flash. This is excellent for a consulting agent, an agent that organizes something for you, or a companion. But there are agents of another kind: those that need to run in the background, transcribe a conversation, live on several platforms simultaneously, and adapt to each one. Such an agent is strong, and you do not build it in a few sentences.

How do you build it? Exactly through the process of this unit: first you go to the world in research, and then you feed the insights into a full guiding prompt, not three lines, but a complete brief that interviews you and produces a research-based agent. This arc is the whole story: from "I built one lightweight assistant" to "I have a system that builds the strong agents for me too." Banay is the step that moves you from the first level to the second.

## Take It to Your World: Building Banay at Home

The build itself is your homework, and that is intentional. I want you to build it yourself, not watch me. The path is precise and waiting for you.

First, make sure all the Deep Research results are in the Files of the Building a Meta-Agent Project, each with its tool name. This is step zero, without this Banay has nothing to lean on.

Now you run two steps, in order. Why two and not one? The same step-by-step discipline from SPOKE™ and Chains: first just to understand, then to build. Do not skip the first step.

### Step 1: Just Summarize and Explain

Go back to the conversation where you wrote the research plan, and run the first prompt. At this stage, only explain the insights, do not build anything yet. Paste it inside the Project:

```
I now need to build a meta-agent - an agent that builds agents.
Look at all the databases and observations I brought you (the Deep
Research results from all the tools in the Files), and summarize all these insights and explain
them to me. At this stage - just explain. Do not build anything yet.
```

It will return all the insights from the research, organized and explained. Go over them, make sure it is what you wanted, and only then move on.

### Step 2: Build Banay with the Full Guiding Prompt

Here we do not use a short prompt. This is the stage that builds the strong agent, and therefore it receives a full guiding prompt, a complete brief that does not jump straight to building but interviews you briefly (which platforms the agent needs to fit, what outputs it needs to produce, how far to go in verification), and then produces a single, complete, and independent system prompt for the meta-agent that builds agents.

Paste all the following text into Claude, and answer the questions it asks. Do not answer in advance, let it interview you:

```
You are going to build a system prompt for a meta-agent for me - an agent whose role is to build other agents, skills, and briefs that guide other systems to build agents. Read the entire brief before you start, and then follow the process below.

## My Context

I have gone through an in-depth Deep Research on how reliable AI agents are built in the world. I am familiar with the patterns (ReAct, Plan-and-Execute, Reflection, Orchestrator-Worker), the mathematics of error accumulation, and the consensus that "we start simple and add complexity only when proven." You do not need to teach me the basics - you need to build me an excellent tool. We can go deep.

## What I Want You to Build

A single, independent system prompt (not a file package, not a skill) that can be pasted into any platform. The meta-agent created from it must:

- Be adaptive - run and generate for Claude web, Cowork, Claude Code, and also web agents / APIs. The anatomy is fixed; the implementation of Tools/Knowledge/Memory/output format changes according to the platform.
- Generate five types of outputs: agent instructions for web, Cowork definition, Claude Code package (SKILL.md / CLAUDE.md / subagents), web agent/API specification, and a guiding brief for another system.
- Know how to build skills as a standalone product as well.
- Perform real tool discovery - in the tools phase, search for existing MCP/connectors and suggest them, do not invent names.
- Work step-by-step with a visible chain of thought, and create documents.
- Decide explicitly, for each agent it builds, whether memory / knowledge / tools are needed - and the default for each is no, added only when an activation condition is met.

## The Process I Want You to Follow

Step 0 - Short interview. Do not dump all the questions on me. Ask one or two at a time, adapt based on my answers, and do not ask about what this brief has already answered. Minimum you must understand: (a) what the role and boundaries of my meta-agent are, (b) which platforms it must support, (c) what types of outputs it must produce, (d) how far I want to go in terms of verification (evals) - from qualitative sanity checks to a full test suite. We will determine the depth of verification and all other depth levels together in the interview.

Step 1 - Direction summary for approval. Present a short summary to me (name, role, target platforms, output types, verification level). Do not continue until I approve.

Step 2 - Research-based design. Work according to "research over assumption" - when you identify a need for a capability, search for the best existing implementation instead of relying on memory:
- Tools: Search for real MCP/connectors (official registries, community libraries). For each tool, write a contract: name, parameters, description, example, side effects, failure behavior.
- Memory: Decision tree - is memory needed between sessions? Progress tracking in a long task? Learning from corrections? Shared with other agents? Or completely stateless? Add only what is required, with a policy of what is kept and what is discarded.
- Knowledge: Distinguish between static knowledge (in context) and runtime-retrieved knowledge; do not flood the context.
- Control pattern: Choose (ReAct as default; Plan-and-Execute for complex tasks), with stop conditions and error handling - never a rigid linear sequence.

Step 3 - Writing the system prompt. Write the meta-agent as a single system prompt. Order of sections = order of the model's attention priority: identity → principles → behavior (interview) → platform adaptivity → build process → anatomy of the agent it populates → tool discovery → output formats → verification → single-agent vs. multi-agent. Phrase positively with reasoning (not just "do not do"), because reasoning prevents the agent from finding loopholes.

> Structure and notation (delimiters): Enclose the system prompt sections in XML tags (for example <identity>, <principles>, <process>) when the target is Claude - this separates instructions from content and reduces leakage between sections. Do not nest more than 2 levels (over-nesting adds noise). The choice is target-dependent: for Claude (web/Cowork/Code), use XML tags; for other environments / web agents, mostly clean Markdown headings. The meta-agent you build should choose the notation based on the target platform - just like all other adaptations, and not just use it in its own system prompt.

Step 4 - Quality gate before delivery. Verify and correct before you deliver:
- [ ] Activation clarity - when the meta-agent acts and when it does not.
- [ ] Least privilege - only the tools/permissions that are actually required; destructive actions require approval.
- [ ] Working contract - there is a planning phase, a verification phase with success criteria, and error recovery paths.
- [ ] Context economy - no generic instructions ("write good code"), no duplications.
- [ ] Persona fidelity - the persona is specific enough to drive autonomous decisions; I will not need to re-explain the task after activation.
- [ ] Anti-pattern check - no "general assistant" framing, no deep XML nesting, no code blocks that should be references.

## The Anatomy the Meta-Agent Will Populate for Every Agent It Builds

(Derived from the research - this is its internal skeleton. It goes over all of them and marks what it included, what it omitted, and why.)

Role+boundaries · Principles/guardrails · Tools (+discovery) · Structured output contract · Knowledge/context · Memory/state · Control loop+stop conditions · Error handling/retry/escalation · guardrails · evals/observability · handoff (only if the agent is a link in a chain).

Minimum reliability gate for any agent (even the simplest): Role+boundaries, stop conditions, error handling. The rest is included as needed.

## Guiding Principles for the Meta-Agent You Build

Start simple - single agent by default; multi-agent only when it is proven that a single agent fails, and then orchestrator + isolated sub-agents that return summaries, single-threaded writing. research over assumption. progressive disclosure. Environment-aware output. Never rely on a capability/tool that has not been verified to exist in the target.

## Your Final Output Format to Me

Deliver one system prompt, readable and editable, in my working language (English, with technical terms in English), but make sure the meta-agent knows how to generate agents in any language. At the end: briefly explain the key design decisions, and offer to run a sanity check on an example task.
```

This is exactly the difference from SPOKE™ / SPOKEN™: there you built a lightweight agent in just a few lines, here the guiding prompt builds a strong agent, based on the proven methods you gathered in your research.

What you will get is not just another agent. It is Banay, the tool that builds all your other tools. From today on, every agent you need, Banay builds. Once it is ready, use it to generate your first agent, and feel the difference for yourself.

### Self-Check

- All Deep Research results are in the Files of the Project before you start.
- You ran the summarize-and-explain step first, and only after that the guiding prompt that builds Banay.
- Banay is ready, and you have built at least one agent with it.

## Bringing It All Together

So, what did we have? The story about the agent that turned out mediocre led to the question: how does the world actually build agents? From that question, the move was born: feed SPOKE™ / SPOKEN™ to Claude and challenge it, ask for a research plan, spread it across several tools in parallel, and gather the results into one Project. And all of this without sitting and waiting, because one thing runs in the background while you work on another.

The big transition is in your head, not in the tool: from "I built an agent" to "I built a tool that builds my tools." SPOKE™ and SPOKEN™ were not thrown away, they are the foundation the meta-agent stands on. And the distinction worth keeping: you build a lightweight agent in a few sentences, you build a strong agent through research and a full guiding prompt.

And one more thing to take with you: the Project that built Banay is not a one-off. If one day the agents coming out of Banay feel weak, you go back there and fix it on the spot. And there is a simple sign you can rely on: a long and detailed system prompt is usually good news, and a short, thin prompt is a red flag that is worth going back and fixing.

Three questions to solidify things:
1. Why do we base the meta-agent on research and not on the method you already knew?
2. What is the advantage of running Deep Research in several tools simultaneously?
3. Why is Banay better suited as a Project rather than just another regular agent?

*(Answers: 1, Because SPOKE / SPOKEN are short and simple, and for a strong agent we want to base it on the proven ways in the world. 2, Each tool accesses different sources, so you get a broad and diverse database. 3, Because it requires a large, one-time knowledge base, and it is better to look at it as a project rather than confusing it with the agents it builds.)*
