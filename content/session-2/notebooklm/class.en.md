# In class — NotebookLM

## ✏️ Your first notebook in NotebookLM

Now it is your turn to try, each of you at your own pace. Set up one notebook, give it one real source, and produce one output from it. The rule is simple: one real source, one output. Not ten sources and not five outputs. One that works is worth more than a pile that confuses.

## The instruction

1. **Pick one source.** A real source from your own work is best: a document, a link, a PDF, a YouTube video, or a recording. And if you do not have one on hand right now, pick a topic from the list below and build a source for it through NotebookLM's quick search (the sources panel → Discover → Fast Research).

2. **Open a new notebook** at notebooklm.google.com and upload the source you chose into it. Wait for the notebook to finish processing it.

3. **Switch the output language to Hebrew** in the notebook settings, and ask one question about the material. Make sure the answer comes back with a marker pointing to your source, and click it to see where it came from.

4. **Open the Studio and ask for one output:** an infographic, a mind map, or a guide. In Hebrew, do not touch Audio and Video, the quality there is weak. Stay with text and visual outputs.

## Five suggested topics (if you do not have a source of your own)

Pick one, run Fast Research on it in Discover, and build a notebook from it:

1. **The perfect coffee at home** — brewing methods, the coffee-to-water ratio, and temperatures.
2. **Healthy sleep** — what really helps you fall asleep, and what is just a myth people keep repeating.
3. **Plants that are hard to kill** — sturdy houseplants, how much water they need, and how much light.
4. **A history of the board game** — how box games evolved, from the classics to today.
5. **A personal budget without the headache** — simple methods to track spending and save.

(All of them are easy to source, and all of them are concrete enough that you will see right away whether the output leans on the source or makes things up.)

## Desired outcome

One notebook, one source that has been processed, one answer with a reference to the source, and one Studio output that leans on that same source. Not a perfect file, but a sketch where you can point to what to take and what not to.

## Challenge (optional)

From your source, produce a second output (say a table if you made a mind map) and compare them: which one organized the material better for you, and why. Or add a second source to the notebook and ask a question that requires both together, and watch the answer point to both sources.
